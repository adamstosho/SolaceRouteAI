Appendix: Detailed Analysis (PART 1)
A: Deep Dive into the Overtourism Challenge
Overtourism arises when visitor volumes surpass a destination’s sustainable limits at a particular period of time (Almawave, 2025; Atzori, 2020). The Big Sur study by Atzori (2020) illustrates that local infrastructure (such as roads, parking, waste) became overloaded as scenic highway traffic surged, causing community outrage. Weber et al. (2017) similarly note that episodic spikes which is often driven by social media hype; can transform a tranquil locale into an unsustainable crush of visitors (Chhikara & Dash, 2024; Palmer, 2025). The effects overtourism is well-documented in Atzori et al. (2020) report “alienated local residents, degraded tourist experiences, and overloaded infrastructure” as common outcomes. Meanwhile, environmental damages (trail erosion, litter, and wildlife disturbance) and cultural dilutions (commodification of heritage) are also endemic concerns (Almawave, 2025; Chhikara & Dash, 2024). Importantly, overtourism is both an environmental and social sustainability problem and it requires balancing economic tourism benefits against community resilience (Atzori, 2020; Chhikara & Dash, 2024).
The severity of overtourism has intensified after COVID as there were international arrivals rebounded to near-record levels by 2024, while travel inspiration now spreads instantly online (Almawave, 2025; Chhikara & Dash, 2024). This makes the challenge not only persistent but urgent. According to Choudhary and Sharma (2024), the tourism industry must contend with “data privacy, over-tourism, and the digital divide” to achieve sustainable growth (). In other words, technological advancement alone cannot solve overtourism unless these socio-technical issues are addressed.
B: Stakeholder Analysis
Regarding the issues of Overtourism, several stakeholders have different interests and pain-points. These stakeholders include the Local Communities and Residents, Tourists (visitors), Tourism Business (like hotels, tour operators, and attractions), Destination Management Organizations (DMO) and Policymakers, and Environmental and Cultural Heritage
•	Local Communities and Residents: The Local Communities and Residents suffer the most from the adverse externalities of Overtourism. It was revealed in the research of Atzori (2020) that residents reported loss of privacy and road congestion that made daily life difficult. In popular cities, locals lament about serious noise, inflated prices/costs, and loss of cultural authenticity (Atzori, 2020; Chhikara & Dash, 2024). Therefore, residents seek controls (e.g. zoning, tourist taxes) to protect quality of life, but also want to benefit from tourism income.
•	Tourists (Visitors): For the tourists, their primary needs are enjoyment, safety, and authenticity. Ironically, Chhikara and Dash (2024) found that tourists’ own behaviour could make the issue worse (such as following social media leads to overcrowding of “Insta-famous” sites). However, not all tourists are indifferent as many of them seek authentic experiences in tranquil settings. These visitors could be receptive to guidance that avoids overtourism and highlights hidden gems (as suggested by Palmer, 2025).
•	Tourism Businesses (Hotels, Tour Operators, Attractions): Tourism-related businesses also rely on consistent tourist spending but face backlash if they seem to take advantage of residents (for example, by charging exorbitant prices) (Almawave, 2025; Chhikara & Dash, 2024). They want solutions that sustain demand without harming the destination’s reputation. For instance, cruise lines and tour operators look for alternatives when ports or sites restrict visits.
•	Destination Management Organizations (DMOs) and Policy Makers: DMOs seek to maximise tourism’s economic benefits while preserving the destination. They must understand and anticipate flows and adjust policies accordingly (Almawave, 2025; Chhikara & Dash, 2024). DMOs, who are frequently underfunded, require efficient tools (data analytics, stakeholder input) to balance competing needs. Policy makers (local governments) also need evidence to support interventions like quota systems.
•	Environment and Cultural Heritage: These are indirect stakeholders (or beneficiaries) often represented by NGOs and local interest groups. They “demand” that tourism be restricted or better managed to protect biodiversity and heritage sites, e.g. in protected national parks or UNESCO sites. (UNESCO, n.d.) As noted by Ardito et al. (2019), natural and cultural sustainability is a key criterion for any tech-driven tourism solution.
In summary, stakeholders range from individual travellers to entire communities. A critical insight from the literature is that tourism is an ecosystem: each stakeholder’s actions affect the others (Atzori, 2020; Palmer, 2025). Effective solutions must therefore align these interests by providing mutual benefits (e.g. shared data platforms accessible to DMOs, businesses, and visitors alike).
C: Review of Existing Solutions and Their Limitations
1.	Regulatory Measures: Many destinations have resorted to traditional control methods. For example, Venice’s entry fee aims to limit day-trip crowding, and Barcelona’s ban on new tourist accommodation seeks to stabilize the housing market (Almawave, 2025). These have had some local success, but such blunt instruments have drawbacks. Limiting numbers can indeed reduce peak demand, but also risk reducing tourism revenue and shifting the problem elsewhere (e.g. Barcelona’s rents fell only to recover as visitors found other housing options). Moreover, these approaches do not guide tourists to alternatives; they merely impose ceilings. Users often react negatively to such caps, feeling their choices are restricted.
2.	Tech-Enabled Monitoring and Analytics: Modern technologies have improved destination management. IoT sensors (cameras, counters) provide DMOs with live data on footfall (Chhikara & Dash, 2024). Big data analytics allows predictions of busy periods by combining flight, accommodation, and event data (Almawave, 2025). For example, VisitScotland’s pilot aggregates hotel occupancy and review data to forecast hotspots in real time (Almawave, 2025). These tools are powerful but underutilized. The information often stays with officials or is reported in dashboards; tourists rarely see it. Thus, analytics alone have limited impact unless they inform operational decisions or public communication. Additionally, sophisticated analytics demand technical expertise that many smaller DMOs lack.
3.	Awareness and Behavioral Campaigns: Education campaigns (e.g. on-site signage, sustainability awards) attempt to change tourist behaviour (Almawave, 2025; Chhikara & Dash, 2024). Some cities incentivise off-peak visits or promote lesser-known attractions (the Japan example is instructive (Chhikara & Dash, 2024)). These strategies leverage “soft” power. However, changing traveler habits is slow. Surveys show many visitors still flock to iconic sites by default. Social media hype often undermines awareness efforts by hyping a single landmark, making comprehensive de-marketing hard to sustain.
4.	Guidance and Personalization: Recent projects are exploring direct communication with tourists. AI chatbots can answer visitor questions, and VR tours can provide alternate experiences (Almawave, 2025; Chhikara & Dash, 2024). But adoption is low so far – very few tourists intentionally use destination apps unless required by local regulations (e.g. health passes). The digital divide also applies here: older visitors or those from regions with weaker mobile coverage may not use such apps.
To sum up, the limitations often stem from fragmentation and lack of feedback loops. Existing solutions either operate at the institutional level (regulation, monitoring) or separately at the consumer level (apps, campaigns), without bridging the two. A 2020 study in Dubrovnik proposed a “digital response system” to automate decongestion policies by linking capacity calculations to actions (Camatti et al., 2020), but this concept remains largely academic (Chuang, 2023; Leal et al., 2025; Rosário & Dias, 2024). In reality, no destination has a unified system that simultaneously guides visitors and initiates adaptive policies based on real-time data (Leal et al., 2025; Ordóñez-Martínez et al., 2024; Rosário & Dias, 2024). This gap is the missing integration or the crux of the opportunity.
D: Gap Identification and Strategic Opportunity
Drawing on the above, the critical gap is actionable connectivity between data and people. Current analytics tell us where and when congestion will occur, but they do not directly influence who goes where. On the other hand, tourists/travelers make trip choices based on static guides or social posts while they are unaware of dynamic conditions. The opportunity is to insert intelligence into this decision process, and it is very important to take note of this.
We specifically identified these unmet needs, which include personalized decision support, two-way information flow and community benefits, as described below:  
•	Personalized Decision Support: Tourists lack a user-friendly tool that alerts them to overcrowding and suggests alternatives.
•	Two-way Information Flow: There is no common platform where tourists’ planned itineraries feed into DMO systems (to improve predictions) and DMO forecasts feedback to tourists.
•	Community Benefits: The small operators and the locals often don’t see direct value from tech solutions. A new system could engage them (e.g. by featuring local experiences or promoting community-based tourism as alternatives).
For instance, let’s imagine a mobile app that not only shows maps and bookings, but also indicates current tourist density at each site (like a “heatmap”) and then automatically recalculates the user’s itinerary to distribute visits. A mathematically similar concept exists in traffic navigation (like Google Maps reroutes drivers to avoid congestion). However, tourism has unique factors which include (a) preferences for certain experiences, (b) timing (peak vs off-peak attractiveness), and (c) multi-mode travel (walking, driving, transit). Our solution can draw from traffic modeling but adapt it for tourism purpose (accounting for visit duration, site capacity, tour groups, among others).
This gap that we identified is supported in the literature. For instance, Choudhary and Sharma (2024) advocate emerging tech (blockchain, AI) to redefine travel experience, implying that smart solutions can fill current gap. The 360info report (Chhikara & Dash 2024) explicitly states that IoT/AI can redirect flows to protect hotspots, but also notes the need to incentivize visits to underutilized areas. Similarly, Palmer (2025) suggests AI could create bespoke (customized) experiences in lesser-known locales to “reduce the harm caused to overcrowded locations”.
Developing a new interactive tool that connects these threads is therefore an important strategic opportunity; and it should be (1) based on smart tourism theory, (2) data-driven, and (3) ethically designed with stakeholder involvements. The next section outlines our proposed digital tool named SolaceRouteAI, which embodies this approach.
E: Digital Tool Concept – SolaceRouteAI
One-line summary: SolaceRouteAI is an AI-powered itinerary planner that dynamically disperses tourists across a region by personalizing travel routes based on real-time density data and user preferences.
How it works: SolaceRouteAI integrates destination data (live visitor counts, weather, and events) with an individual’s profile (interests, mobility constraints). When a user specifies desired attractions or arrival times, the system evaluates current and predicted crowd levels (using machine learning models trained on historical data (Almawave, 2025; Palmer, 2025)). It then adjusts the itinerary – for example, suggesting that a user visit a less-crowded museum first, or delay a visit to a major landmark until later. All suggestions are rationalized (the app explains, “We suggest the botanical garden now because the cathedral is at capacity and will clear up later”). Users can give feedback (thumbs up/down) to refine preferences, ensuring the system learns over time.
Stakeholder advantages: Tourists get tailored, hassle-free itineraries that help them avoid crowds and discover new experiences, aligning with their interests. DMOs gain anonymized data on shifting demand and can communicate directly with visitors (via the app) during congestion peaks. Local businesses and residents benefit because SolaceRouteAI can include community-run attractions in alternative itineraries, distributing economic benefits more evenly. Importantly, the tool would employ strict data privacy safeguards, i.e. only aggregated flow data and opt-in user preferences are used, addressing concerns about surveillance (Choudhary & Sharma, 2024). Finally, SolaceRouteAI is device-agnostic (accessible via web (preferably Progressive Web Application [PWA]) or app) and multilingual, to bridge digital divide issues for international visitors and those without high-end smartphones.
